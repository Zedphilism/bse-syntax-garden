# Analog Signal Is a Continuously Varying Physical Quantity with Infinite Possible Values

An analog signal is a physical quantity — voltage, current, sound pressure, temperature — that changes smoothly and continuously over time, taking any value within a range rather than snapping between fixed levels.

## Explanation

In nature, most phenomena are analog. A microphone output, for example, follows the continuous variations in air pressure caused by sound. The signal can take any voltage between its minimum and maximum, with infinitely fine gradations between them.

This continuity is both an advantage and a liability. Analog signals can represent nuance, but they are also susceptible to noise: any interference adds directly to the signal and cannot be distinguished from the intended information. This is why digital systems, which snap to only two valid levels, are preferred for storing and transmitting information reliably.

## Key Points

- Continuous range of values (infinite resolution in theory)
- Susceptible to cumulative noise and distortion
- Natural phenomena (sound, light, temperature) are inherently analog

## Example

A vinyl record groove encodes audio as a continuous physical waveform. The stylus follows every microscopic variation, reproducing all the noise and wear alongside the music — a characteristic analog limitation.
